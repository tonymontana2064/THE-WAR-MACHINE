§ I

The American Arsenal: Origins, Power & Ownership

The United States defense-industrial complex is the most powerful, most capitalized military supply chain in human history. Five prime contractors dominate: Lockheed Martin, RTX (Raytheon), Northrop Grumman, General Dynamics, and Boeing. Together, they absorb the lion's share of the Pentagon's $961.6 billion FY2026 budget request and provide the weapons now being expended over Iranian skies at approximately $1.43 billion per day.

These are not merely contractors — they are the strategic backbone of American geopolitical power projection. When CENTCOM identifies a target in Tehran, the missiles that strike it are Tomahawks built by RTX. The aircraft delivering precision strikes are F-35s built by Lockheed Martin. The intelligence feeding those missions flows through Palantir's AI platforms. The war, in a very real sense, is a product demo.

🇺🇸 United States · NYSE: LMT
Lockheed Martin
World's Largest Defense Contractor
Founded: 1995 merger of Lockheed Corp (est. 1912 by Allan & Malcolm Loughead) and Martin Marietta (est. 1961). The Skunk Works facility in Palmdale, California, has birthed every major classified aircraft program since WWII — the U-2, SR-71 Blackbird, F-117 Nighthawk, and F-22 Raptor.

Primary Systems: F-35 Lightning II (world's costliest weapons program at $1.7 trillion lifecycle), F-22 Raptor, C-130J Hercules, Black Hawk helicopter, THAAD missile defense, PAC-3 MSE interceptors, HIMARS, Trident II D5 submarine-launched ballistic missiles, hypersonic glide vehicles.

Active in This War: F-35s leading strike packages over Iran. PAC-3 MSE interceptors defending against Iranian ballistic missiles. THAAD providing theater-level defense. Lockheed signed a 7-year deal in January 2026 to triple PAC-3 production to 2,000/year and quadruple THAAD to 400/year.

The Israeli Arsenal: Small Nation, Global Edge

Israel operates the world's most battle-tested defense industrial ecosystem per capita. With a population of 9 million facing existential threats since 1948, Israeli defense companies have been forced to innovate under live-fire conditions that no sandbox exercise can replicate. Systems like Iron Dome, Arrow, David's Sling, and Merkava represent the highest density of real-world-validated technology on Earth. In January 2026, the Israeli government formalized plans to partially privatize IAI (valued at ~$20B) and Rafael (~$10B) through IPOs on the Tel Aviv Stock Exchange.

🇮🇱 Israel · NASDAQ: ESLT

Israel's Premier Private Defense Contractor
Founded: 1966, incorporated from earlier defense electronics work. Became dominant after acquiring Israel Military Industries (IMI) from the state in 2018 — a purchase valued at approximately 4× its original acquisition cost by 2026. Headquartered in Haifa.

Primary Systems: Hermes drone family, Iron Fist active protection system for Eitan APC and M113, Iron Sting laser-guided mortar, PULS rocket artillery, Helmet Display and Tracking System (HDTS) for IDF Black Hawks, Skylark III mini-UAV, Spear EW electronic warfare suites, C4ISR systems, night-vision and EO/IR sensors, cyber intelligence platforms, DIRCM countermeasures.

Structure: Publicly traded (NASDAQ/TASE). ~80% of revenue from international customers — one of the most export-oriented defense companies globally. Operates five segments: Aerospace; C4I & Cyber; ISTAR & EW; Land; and Elbit Systems of America (ESA). Backlog as of Q3 2025: $25.2 billion.

Active in This War: Drone reconnaissance; Iron Fist protecting IDF vehicles; C4ISR systems coordinating Israeli airstrikes on Iran; Elbit upgraded to "Strong Buy" by analysts on March 3 citing Israeli defense budget increase of ~$2.9 billion.

Iran's Arsenal: The Sanctioned War Machine

Iran's defense industry is arguably the most remarkable case study in strategic self-sufficiency under duress in modern history. Facing total Western arms embargoes since 1979, and UN weapons sanctions since 2007, Iran built a military-industrial complex of over 300 entities under the Defense Industries Organization (DIO) umbrella — producing ballistic missiles, drones, submarines, frigates, and increasingly sophisticated electronic warfare systems with minimal foreign input. Iran has fired over 500 ballistic missiles and nearly 2,000 drones since February 28. That is their defense industry's output on display. The IDF now reports that Israeli strikes have severely damaged Iran's ballistic missile production, to the point that it "currently does not have the ability to manufacture any new missiles."

🇮🇷 Iran · State (MODAFL)
Defense Industries Organization (DIO)
The Umbrella Conglomerate — 300+ Entities
Founded: Formally consolidated in 1981 during the Iran-Iraq War, when the new revolutionary government unified disparate military factories under a single command. The IRGC was placed in charge of reorganizing it. Origins trace to the Shah's Military Industries Organization (MIO) of 1963 and earlier Shah-era arms factories building German-licensed weapons from the 1940s onward.

Primary Products: Ammunition and artillery (Armament Industries Group — gun howitzers, multiple rocket launchers, sniper rifles, machine guns), armor plating, explosives, military vehicles, light weapons manufacturing across 300+ subsidiary entities. DIO is the logistics and manufacturing backbone for all ground forces.

Strategic Role: Oversees all production, R&D, and procurement under the Ministry of Defense and Armed Forces Logistics (MODAFL). The DIO director is ultimately accountable to the President. Has 35,000+ employees, 30% of whom are university graduates. Sanctioned by UN Security Council (2007) and U.S. Treasury (Executive Order 13382) for WMD program support.

§ VI

The Global Defense Market: Size, Demand & Trajectory

$2.75T 2026 Market

Scale of the Market

The global defense market is estimated at $2.75 trillion in 2026, projected to reach $4.26 trillion by 2035 at a 5% CAGR. World military expenditure reached $2.718 trillion in 2024 — a 9.4% increase, the steepest year-on-year rise since at least 1988. The top 100 arms-producing companies earned $679 billion in 2024 revenues, the highest since SIPRI began tracking in 2002. North America alone accounts for $336 billion (49%) of top-100 arms revenues, with the U.S. fielding 39 of those 100 companies.

Demand Drivers

Russia-Ukraine war (now 4+ years), Israel-Iran conflict, China-Taiwan tensions, NATO's commitment to raise spending from 2% to 5% of GDP by 2035, the Golden Dome missile defense project (~$175B, $25B initial), and the Iran war's $50B emergency supplemental are all structurally elevating demand. The Pentagon's FY2026 budget request is $961.6 billion in base funding — unprecedented.

Supply Constraints

§ VIII

How Future Wars Will Be Fought: The 2026 Lessons

1. The Drone-Interceptor Asymmetry Will Define 21st Century Warfare

Iran's strategy of using ~$20,000 Shahed-136 drones against $2–5M interceptors exposed a fundamental economic vulnerability in Western air defense architecture. The 106:1 cost ratio is unsustainable at scale. Every military watching this conflict — China, Russia, North Korea, Turkey, India, Pakistan — has absorbed this lesson. The race to develop cost-effective directed-energy weapons (lasers, high-power microwave) and autonomous counter-drone systems (swarm-on-swarm) is now the defining procurement priority.

2. Decapitation Strategy — Political Leadership Is the Target

The elimination of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei in the opening strike of Operation Epic Fury on February 28 marks one of the most consequential single acts in modern warfare. Every nuclear-armed and aspiring-nuclear state — Iran's successors, North Korea, Russia — has now watched the U.S.-Israel axis demonstrate willingness to kill head-of-state targets directly. Defense doctrine worldwide will now explicitly incorporate leadership bunker hardening, dispersal, and succession protocol as tier-one priorities.

3. Space Is Now the Decisive Domain

U.S. Space Force orbital sensors detected Iranian ballistic missile launches within milliseconds. SpaceX's Starlink/Starshield constellations provided unjammable communications for autonomous drone swarms. Israel's Ofek-class reconnaissance satellites enabled real-time targeting. For the first time, a space-integrated, AI-driven command-and-control architecture operated as the cognitive layer of a major regional war. Countries without indigenous space capabilities have observed their own strategic blindness in sharp relief.

4. AI Targeting at Scale Is Here

Palantir's Maven Smart System and AIP platform processed multi-source intelligence to generate target lists at machine speed. IDF Unit 8200 and U.S. Cyber Command executed coordinated offensive cyber operations against Iranian military telecommunications in the opening hours. This is the first major conflict where AI-generated targeting was central to the opening strike package — not a supplement, but the primary decision architecture.

5. Chokepoint Economics: The Strait of Hormuz Will Never Be Taken for Granted Again

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of global oil transits — sent Brent crude from $70 to $119.50/barrel and wiped $3.5 trillion from global equity markets in a week. Every nation's energy security doctrine, every shipping insurer's actuarial model, and every central bank's inflation framework has been permanently recalibrated. The lesson: the world's most critical chokepoint is a weapon that can be weaponized by a state with nothing left to lose.

6. Nations Watching & Learning Most Intensely

China is studying the decapitation doctrine and space-integrated ISR for Taiwan contingencies. Russia is studying drone saturation tactics and Western munitions drawdown rates (which now reduce NATO inventories for Ukraine). India and Pakistan are reassessing their bilateral conflict scenarios through the drone-cost lens. Saudi Arabia and UAE, having suffered Iranian retaliation strikes on their airports and oil infrastructure, are accelerating THAAD, Patriot, and their own domestic defense industrial programs. North Korea is the most dangerous student — watching regime survival under simultaneous air and cyber assault with nuclear weapons in the background.
